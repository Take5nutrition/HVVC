The Philosophy

Build a Better Athlete

The core goal is not to throw heavy weights at young athletes. The goal is to teach them how to move, control their body, and build a strong foundation that can grow with them as their athletic career develops.

This is not volleyball skills training — we're not coaching their swing or serve. We're training the physical building blocks of athleticism so that their strength, speed, and power transfer better into the game.

Led by Tatum Erickson — NASM Performance Enhancement Specialist, Nutrition Therapy Master, and BS candidate in Sports & Exercise Science.

Get Started
Layer 1 — Foundation

Learning to Move First

Before we build power, we build the foundation. Every athlete starts here — no matter their level.

Core Movement Patterns

Squatting, hinging, pushing, pulling, and bracing. These are the fundamental patterns every athlete needs — and most haven't been taught properly.

Single-Leg Strength

Running, cutting, landing, and jumping all happen one leg at a time. We build knee stability and single-leg control to keep athletes healthy and powerful.

Tendon & Joint Strength

Isometric holds build tendon and connective tissue strength, improve joint stability, and develop real strength without excessive stress on the body.

Enjoyment & Buy-In

Training should be challenging and rewarding. Athletes leave sessions feeling confident, not beaten down. Forced workouts create resistance — not progress.

Layer 2 — Sport Specificity

Built for Volleyball

Once the foundation is in place, training shifts toward the specific physical demands of volleyball. This means training the qualities that actually show up in a match.

  • Elastic ankle stiffness — reactive power for blocks, cuts, and dives
  • Lateral first-step quickness — faster passing adjustments and defensive reads
  • Penultimate braking — cleaner, more powerful approach jumps
  • Scapular upward rotation — healthy shoulder mechanics for hitting all season

Athletes are tested every 6 weeks to track real progress throughout the year.

A Note for Parents

Is Strength Training Safe for Kids?

There's a long-standing concern that strength training too early causes stunted growth or damages the spine. These claims have been debunked many times over — but that doesn't mean we're loading young athletes with a heavy barbell.

We take athletes through a slow, earned progression. Bodyweight squat → goblet squat → single-leg work → weighted variations. No spinal loading until the foundation is built and mastery is demonstrated — consistently, over time.
